(Still) Life

I have never been the biggest fan of still life paintings. In my “local” museum, Cambridge’s Fitzwilliam, the first room in the upstairs gallery is a self-proclaimed “Flower Room”, the walls lined from floor to ceiling with technically impeccable depictions of flower vases. I have always liked this room, largely for the overall impression it gives, the way you can lose yourself in the uniformity and simplicity. Take a closer look, however, and I’m almost immediately bored. All the paintings look the same. While their technique and skill are faultless, these painting don’t make me feel very much, and what’s the point of art if it doesn’t make you feel, right?

We painted a lot of still life in school for this very purpose: it develops important skills within a controlled environment, that allows an artist to develop their understanding of several important aspects of painting. As the artist, you set up the composition, meaning that you decide on the light, the objects, the difficulty, as well as creating through the image a narrative, theme or colour harmony. Still life is not a simple snapshot of some objects lying around, it is often carefully orchestrated, it can mean something just as a portrait might. Some still life paintings worth mentioning are Paul Cezanne’s Still Life with Sugar, Caravaggio’s Basket of Fruit, Vincent Van Gogh’s Sunflowers and John Singer Sargent’s Roses. I’ll focus on each in turn. Cezanne’s Still Life carries with it a distinct sense of movement beyond the frame, the tablecloth is rumpled beneath the fruit, the scene feels rushed and quite technically inaccurate in terms of its use of perspective. It is an impression fit for a (post)impressionist. Caravaggio is, in contrast, far more technically accurate. His level of detail is immaculate, if a little harshly defined against the plain background. This reminds me of the flower paintings, although I do feel a twinge of something looking at it, an admiration of the Baroque perfection, perhaps. Sunflowers needs no further introduction. If you were ever to feel something looking at a still life, you would feel it with this. What makes Sunflowers so compelling is not just the fact that we know the artist (as Van Gogh’s own life and history are doubtlessly a powerful emotional pull to his paintings), but it is that we feel as if we truly know the subject. When I look at this painting, I can see the moment it’s captured, the flowers that will doubtlessly shrivel and die beyond the frame, engendering a melancholy beauty to their proud heads. Sargent’s Roses are just a step beyond. The beautifully composed handful of roses lie on the edge of a desk, they are almost decaying before our eyes.

There, then, is the beauty I have found in still life. For a moment, they stop time, they stop decay, they stop life. They exist in a moment where the flower is forever in bloom, the plum always ripe, the apple always browning. All those flowers in the Fitzwilliam died a very long time ago, and every time a curious visitor steps into Gallery 17, they are born again.

The paintings throughout this article are attempts of still life from a holiday in Wales in 2025. They are not technically admirable, rely far more on bold colour and contrast than they do on meaning or vision. They are painted with gouache, each completed within a two-hour period (the time it takes to watch a film) and inspired not by objects before me, but rather hastily found Pinterest pics. They did, however, give me a little taste of still life splendour, provided a drive to try more, to compose my own images, and to depict life even when it’s standing still.
